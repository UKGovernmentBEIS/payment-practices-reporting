What you need to report

Start and end date of reporting period

These are shown above, under 'Your reporting periods and deadlines'

Average time to pay in days

Of all the payments you made in the reporting period, the average time it took to pay. Only include payments that you have made during the reporting period. A payment is counted as 'made' when it is available to the supplier, (for example, when it clears their bank account) unless delayed by unforeseen circumstances.

Percentage of invoices due which werenâ€™t paid within the agreed period

This relates to the number of invoices, not their value. Count all invoices where the contractually agreed date for payment was within the reporting period. This includes unpaid and disputed invoices.

Percentage of invoices paid

  • within 30 days
  • in 31 to 60 days
  • in 61 days or more

Only include payments that you have made during the reporting period.

Standard payment terms

This must include a description of your standard payment terms. If you have different terms for different types of supplier, you should explain them. Most companies write a couple of paragraphs here.

Standard payment period

This is your standard contractual payment period in days.

Your maximum contractual payment period

This is the longest payment period agreed with a supplier in a contract signed during the reporting period. You can just put the number of days, or you can add an explanation.

Changes to your standard terms

If you changed your standard terms during the reporting period, include details of exactly what has changed: be sure to include the 'before' and 'after' situation. You must also state whether you consulted or notified suppliers about any changes, and how you did this. Most businesses write a couple of paragraphs here.

Your dispute resolution process

Describe how you respond to complaints from suppliers, and how you resolve payment disputes. Most businesses write a couple of paragraphs here.

Does your business offer e-invoicing?

This is where suppliers can electronically submit and track invoices. It's not just allowing suppliers to email you an invoice.

Does your business offer supply chain finance options?

This is where you have an arrangement where a supplier who has submitted an invoice can be paid by a third-party finance provider earlier than the agreed payment date. Your business would then pay the finance provider the invoiced sum by the payment date.
You can give more details about this in your information about your standard payment terms, but you don't have to.

Under its payment practices and policies, can your business deduct sums from payments as a charge for remaining on a supplier list?

Did the business do this during this reporting period?

You must answer this even if you answered 'no' to the above question.

Has your business signed up to a code of conduct or standards on payment practices?

For example, the Prompt Payment Code

Before you start

The person publishing the report needs to have a Companies House authentication code to publish reports for your company (the same code you'd use to file your annual accounts online. The code is sent to your company's registered office by post, so allow plenty of time for delivery.
